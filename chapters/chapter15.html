Environment is everything that surrounds us. In this chapter, we will learn about ecosystems, food chains, environmental problems, and how to protect our environment.

Ecosystem - What are its Components?

What is an Ecosystem?

Definition: An ecosystem is a system formed by the interaction of all living organisms with each other and with the physical and chemical factors of the environment in which they live.

Components of Ecosystem:
  • Biotic Components: Living organisms (plants, animals, microorganisms)
  • Abiotic Components: Non-living factors (air, water, soil, temperature, light)

Types of Ecosystems

1. Natural Ecosystems:
  • Terrestrial: Forests, grasslands, deserts
  • Aquatic: Ponds, lakes, rivers, oceans
2. Artificial Ecosystems:
  • Crop fields
  • Gardens
  • Aquariums
  • Parks

Functions of Ecosystem

  • Energy flow through food chains
  • Nutrient cycling
  • Maintenance of ecological balance
  • Provision of habitat for organisms
  • Climate regulation

Food Chains and Webs

Food Chain

Definition: A food chain is a linear sequence of organisms through which nutrients and energy pass as one organism eats another.

Components of Food Chain:
  • Producers: Green plants that make their own food (autotrophs)
  • Primary Consumers: Herbivores that eat plants
  • Secondary Consumers: Carnivores that eat herbivores
  • Tertiary Consumers: Top carnivores that eat other carnivores
  • Decomposers: Organisms that break down dead organic matter
Example of Food Chain:

Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle

Food Web

Definition: A food web is a network of interconnected food chains showing the feeding relationships between organisms.

Characteristics:
  • More complex than food chains
  • Shows multiple feeding relationships
  • More stable than food chains
  • Better represents natural ecosystems

Energy Flow in Ecosystem

10% Law (Lindeman's Law):

Only 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. 90% is lost as heat, respiration, and waste.

Pyramid of Energy:
  • Producers have maximum energy
  • Energy decreases at each trophic level
  • Top consumers have minimum energy

How do our Activities Affect the Environment?

1. Air Pollution

Causes:
  • Burning of fossil fuels
  • Industrial emissions
  • Vehicle exhaust
  • Burning of garbage
Effects:
  • Respiratory diseases
  • Acid rain
  • Global warming
  • Ozone layer depletion

2. Water Pollution

Causes:
  • Industrial waste disposal
  • Sewage discharge
  • Agricultural runoff
  • Oil spills
Effects:
  • Water-borne diseases
  • Death of aquatic organisms
  • Contamination of drinking water
  • Eutrophication

3. Soil Pollution

Causes:
  • Excessive use of pesticides
  • Industrial waste dumping
  • Mining activities
  • Deforestation
Effects:
  • Loss of soil fertility
  • Contamination of crops
  • Groundwater pollution
  • Soil erosion

4. Noise Pollution

Causes:
  • Traffic noise
  • Industrial machinery
  • Construction activities
  • Loudspeakers
Effects:
  • Hearing loss
  • Stress and anxiety
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Cardiovascular problems

Ozone Layer and its Depletion

What is Ozone Layer?

Definition: The ozone layer is a protective layer of ozone (O₃) gas in the stratosphere that absorbs harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun.

Importance:
  • Protects living organisms from UV radiation
  • Prevents skin cancer
  • Protects eyes from cataracts
  • Maintains ecological balance

Ozone Layer Depletion

Causes:
  • CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons): Used in refrigerators, air conditioners
  • HCFCs: Hydrochlorofluorocarbons
  • Halons: Used in fire extinguishers
  • Methyl bromide: Used in pesticides
Effects:
  • Increased UV radiation reaching earth
  • Skin cancer and sunburn
  • Cataracts and eye damage
  • Weakened immune system
  • Damage to marine life
  • Reduced crop yields

Montreal Protocol

Definition: An international treaty signed in 1987 to protect the ozone layer by phasing out the production of ozone-depleting substances.

Measures:
  • Ban on CFC production
  • Use of ozone-friendly alternatives
  • Regular monitoring of ozone layer
  • Public awareness campaigns

Managing the Garbage we Produce

Types of Waste

1. Biodegradable Waste:
  • Organic waste that can be decomposed
  • Examples: Food waste, paper, leaves
  • Can be composted
  • Environment-friendly
2. Non-biodegradable Waste:
  • Waste that cannot be decomposed
  • Examples: Plastic, glass, metals
  • Cause environmental pollution
  • Need special disposal methods

Waste Management Methods

1. Reduce:
  • Minimize waste generation
  • Use reusable products
  • Avoid unnecessary packaging
2. Reuse:
  • Use items multiple times
  • Donate usable items
  • Repair instead of replacing
3. Recycle:
  • Convert waste into new products
  • Separate waste for recycling
  • Support recycling programs
4. Composting:
  • Convert organic waste to manure
  • Reduces landfill waste
  • Provides natural fertilizer

Plastic Waste Management

Problems with Plastic:
  • Non-biodegradable
  • Chokes drains and water bodies
  • Harmful to animals
  • Releases toxic chemicals
Solutions:
  • Use cloth/jute bags
  • Carry water bottles
  • Use paper/glass containers
  • Proper disposal in designated bins

Environmental Conservation

Ways to Protect Environment:

  • Plant Trees: Increase green cover
  • Save Water: Fix leaks, use water efficiently
  • Save Energy: Use energy-efficient appliances
  • Use Public Transport: Reduce vehicle emissions
  • Proper Waste Disposal: Follow 3R principle
  • Use Renewable Energy: Solar, wind power
  • Educate Others: Spread awareness
  • Support Conservation: Join environmental groups

Important Definitions

Ecosystem: A system formed by the interaction of all living organisms with each other and with the physical and chemical factors of the environment
Food Chain: A linear sequence of organisms through which nutrients and energy pass as one organism eats another
Food Web: A network of interconnected food chains showing the feeding relationships between organisms
Biodegradable: Substances that can be broken down by microorganisms
Non-biodegradable: Substances that cannot be broken down by microorganisms
Ozone Layer: A protective layer of ozone gas in the stratosphere that absorbs harmful UV radiation
Pollution: The presence of harmful substances in the environment that cause adverse effects

Important Points to Remember

  • Ecosystem has both biotic and abiotic components
  • Food chains show energy flow in ecosystem
  • Only 10% energy is transferred to next trophic level
  • Ozone layer protects from UV radiation
  • CFCs cause ozone layer depletion
  • Plastic is non-biodegradable and harmful
  • 3R principle: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle
  • Biodegradable waste can be composted
  • Montreal Protocol protects ozone layer
  • Environmental conservation is everyone's responsibility

Sample Questions

Q1. What is an ecosystem? What are its components?

Answer: An ecosystem is a system formed by the interaction of all living organisms with each other and with the physical and chemical factors of the environment. Its components are biotic (living organisms) and abiotic (non-living factors like air, water, soil).

Q2. What is a food chain? Give an example.

Answer: A food chain is a linear sequence of organisms through which nutrients and energy pass as one organism eats another. Example: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle

Q3. What is ozone layer depletion? What are its causes?

Answer: Ozone layer depletion is the thinning of the ozone layer in the stratosphere. Its main causes are CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons), HCFCs, halons, and methyl bromide used in refrigerators, air conditioners, and fire extinguishers.

Q4. What is the difference between biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste?

Answer: Biodegradable waste can be broken down by microorganisms into simpler substances (e.g., food waste, paper). Non-biodegradable waste cannot be broken down by microorganisms and persists in the environment (e.g., plastic, glass, metals).

Q5. What are the 3R principles of waste management?

Answer: The 3R principles are: 1) Reduce - minimize waste generation, 2) Reuse - use items multiple times, 3) Recycle - convert waste into new products.
