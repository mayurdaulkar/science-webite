1. Chemical Reactions and their Characteristics

Chemical Reaction

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances (reactants) are transformed into one or more different substances (products) with different properties.

Characteristics of Chemical Reactions:

  • Change in State: Solid to liquid, liquid to gas, etc.
  • Change in Color: Formation of new colored substances
  • Evolution of Gas: Bubbles or gas formation
  • Change in Temperature: Exothermic or endothermic reactions
  • Formation of Precipitate: Insoluble solid formation
Example:

When iron nails are placed in copper sulphate solution, the blue color of the solution fades and a brown deposit forms on the iron nails. This indicates a chemical reaction has occurred.

2. Chemical Equations and their Balancing

Chemical Equation

A chemical equation is a symbolic representation of a chemical reaction using chemical formulae of reactants and products.

Law of Conservation of Mass

In a chemical reaction, the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products. This means the number of atoms of each element must be the same on both sides of the equation.

Steps to Balance Chemical Equations:

  1. Write the unbalanced equation
  2. Count atoms of each element on both sides
  3. Add coefficients to balance atoms
  4. Check that all atoms are balanced
Example: Balancing the equation
Unbalanced: Fe + H₂SO₄ → Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + H₂
Balanced: 2Fe + 3H₂SO₄ → Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + 3H₂

3. Types of Chemical Reactions

1. Combination Reactions

Two or more substances combine to form a single product.

A + B → AB
Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

2. Decomposition Reactions

A single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances.

AB → A + B
Example: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂

3. Displacement Reactions

One element displaces another element from its compound.

A + BC → AC + B
Example: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu

4. Double Displacement Reactions

Two compounds exchange their ions to form new compounds.

AB + CD → AD + CB
Example: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃

4. Oxidation and Reduction Reactions

Oxidation

Addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen from a substance.

Reduction

Removal of oxygen or addition of hydrogen to a substance.

Redox Reactions

Reactions in which oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously are called redox reactions.

Example:
CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O
Oxidation: H₂ → H₂O (addition of oxygen)
Reduction: CuO → Cu (removal of oxygen)

5. Effects of Oxidation in Daily Life

1. Corrosion

Gradual destruction of materials (usually metals) by chemical reaction with their environment.

Example:

Rusting of iron: 4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃

2. Rancidity

Oxidation of fats and oils in food, leading to unpleasant taste and smell.

Prevention Methods:

  • Adding antioxidants to food
  • Storing food in airtight containers
  • Refrigeration
  • Adding nitrogen gas to food packages

Important Formulas and Definitions

Key Points to Remember

  • Chemical equations must be balanced to satisfy the law of conservation of mass
  • In redox reactions, oxidation and reduction occur together
  • Corrosion is a slow oxidation process
  • Rancidity can be prevented by antioxidants
  • Physical changes are reversible, chemical changes are not

Common Chemical Equations:

  • Photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O
  • Burning of methane: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
  • Formation of water: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
