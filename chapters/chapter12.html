1. Electric Current and Circuit

Electric current is the flow of electric charge through a conductor.

Definition: Electric Current

The rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor.

Current Formula:

I = Q/t

Where: I = current (A), Q = charge (C), t = time (s)

Diagram: Simple Electric Circuit

2. Electric Potential and Potential Difference

Electric potential is the work done per unit charge to bring a charge from infinity to a point.

Definition: Potential Difference

The work done per unit charge in moving a charge between two points.

Potential Difference:

V = W/Q

Where: V = potential difference (V), W = work done (J), Q = charge (C)

Units:

  • Voltage: Volt (V)
  • Current: Ampere (A)
  • Resistance: Ohm (Ω)
  • Power: Watt (W)

3. Ohm's Law

Ohm's law states that current through a conductor is directly proportional to voltage.

Definition: Ohm's Law

At constant temperature, the current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it.

Ohm's Law Formula:

V = IR

Where: V = voltage (V), I = current (A), R = resistance (Ω)

Diagram: Ohm's Law Graph

4. Factors Affecting Resistance

Resistance depends on material, length, area, and temperature.

Resistance Formula:

R = ρL/A

Where: R = resistance (Ω), ρ = resistivity (Ωm), L = length (m), A = area (m²)

Factors Affecting Resistance:

  • Length: R ∝ L (directly proportional)
  • Area: R ∝ 1/A (inversely proportional)
  • Material: Different materials have different resistivity
  • Temperature: Resistance changes with temperature

5. Resistors in Series and Parallel

Resistors can be connected in series or parallel combinations.

Diagram: Resistor Combinations

Series Resistance:

R = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...

Parallel Resistance:

1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...

6. Heating Effect of Electric Current

When current flows through a conductor, it produces heat due to resistance.

Definition: Joule's Law

The heat produced in a conductor is directly proportional to the square of current, resistance, and time.

Heat Produced:

H = I²Rt

Where: H = heat (J), I = current (A), R = resistance (Ω), t = time (s)

Example: Electric Heater

An electric heater with resistance 50Ω draws current 4A for 2 hours. Heat produced = 4² × 50 × 7200 = 5,760,000 J.

7. Electric Power

Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed.

Definition: Electric Power

The rate at which electrical energy is consumed or transferred.

Power Formula:

P = VI = I²R = V²/R

Where: P = power (W), V = voltage (V), I = current (A), R = resistance (Ω)

Power Units:

  • Watt (W) = 1 joule/second
  • Kilowatt (kW) = 1000 watts
  • Horsepower (hp) = 746 watts
