1. Bonding in Carbon - The Covalent Bond

Carbon has four electrons in its outermost shell and needs four more electrons to complete its octet. It can achieve this by sharing electrons with other atoms, forming covalent bonds.

Definition: Covalent Bond

A chemical bond formed by the sharing of electrons between atoms.

Important Points:

  • Carbon forms four covalent bonds
  • Carbon can form single, double, or triple bonds
  • Carbon can bond with other carbon atoms
  • Carbon can bond with hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and halogens

2. Versatile Nature of Carbon

Carbon shows versatile nature due to:

  • Catenation: Ability to form bonds with other carbon atoms
  • Tetravalency: Ability to form four bonds
  • Multiple bonding: Can form single, double, and triple bonds

Examples of Carbon Compounds:

  • Methane: CH₄
  • Ethane: C₂H₆
  • Ethene: C₂H₄
  • Ethyne: C₂H₂

3. Saturated and Unsaturated Carbon Compounds

Definition: Saturated Compounds

Carbon compounds in which carbon atoms are connected by single bonds only.

Definition: Unsaturated Compounds

Carbon compounds in which carbon atoms are connected by double or triple bonds.

Examples:

  • Saturated: Methane (CH₄), Ethane (C₂H₆)
  • Unsaturated: Ethene (C₂H₄), Ethyne (C₂H₂)

4. Homologous Series

A series of compounds in which the same functional group substitutes for hydrogen in a carbon chain.

Characteristics of Homologous Series:

  • Same general formula
  • Similar chemical properties
  • Physical properties change gradually
  • Difference of CH₂ between consecutive members

5. Chemical Properties of Carbon Compounds

Combustion

Combustion Reactions:

  • CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
  • C₂H₅OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O

Oxidation

Carbon compounds can be oxidized to form carboxylic acids.

Addition Reaction

Unsaturated compounds can add hydrogen in the presence of catalysts.

Example: Hydrogenation of Ethene

C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆ (in presence of Ni catalyst)

6. Some Important Carbon Compounds

Ethanol (C₂H₅OH)

  • Also called ethyl alcohol
  • Used as solvent and in alcoholic beverages
  • Can be produced by fermentation of sugars

Ethanoic Acid (CH₃COOH)

  • Also called acetic acid
  • Main component of vinegar
  • Used in food preservation

7. Soaps and Detergents

Soaps are sodium or potassium salts of long chain carboxylic acids.

Definition: Saponification

The process of making soap by the hydrolysis of fats and oils with alkali.

Soap Formation:

Fat/Oil + NaOH → Soap + Glycerol

Properties of Soaps:

  • Have hydrophilic (water-loving) and hydrophobic (oil-loving) parts
  • Form micelles in water
  • Work well in soft water
  • Do not work well in hard water
