1. Reflection of Light

Reflection is the phenomenon of bouncing back of light when it strikes a surface.

Definition: Reflection

The phenomenon in which light rays bounce back from a surface after striking it.

Laws of Reflection:

  • Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection
  • Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal lie in the same plane

Diagram: Reflection of Light

2. Spherical Mirrors

Spherical mirrors are mirrors with curved surfaces that can be concave or convex.

Definition: Spherical Mirror

A mirror whose reflecting surface is a part of a sphere.

Types of Spherical Mirrors:

  • Concave Mirror: Curved inward, converges light
  • Convex Mirror: Curved outward, diverges light

Diagram: Spherical Mirrors

Mirror Formula:

1/f = 1/u + 1/v

Where: f = focal length, u = object distance, v = image distance

Example: Concave Mirror

If an object is placed 20 cm from a concave mirror of focal length 10 cm, the image distance can be calculated using the mirror formula.

3. Refraction of Light

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one medium to another.

Definition: Refraction

The bending of light when it passes from one transparent medium to another.

Laws of Refraction:

  • Incident ray, refracted ray, and normal lie in the same plane
  • sin i / sin r = constant (Snell's law)

Diagram: Refraction of Light

4. Refraction by Spherical Lenses

Lenses are transparent materials with curved surfaces that refract light.

Definition: Lens

A transparent material with curved surfaces that refracts light to form images.

Types of Lenses:

  • Convex Lens: Converges light, forms real and virtual images
  • Concave Lens: Diverges light, forms only virtual images

Diagram: Types of Lenses

Lens Formula:

1/f = 1/u + 1/v

Where: f = focal length, u = object distance, v = image distance

5. Refraction of Light through a Prism

A prism is a transparent object with triangular cross-section that disperses light.

Definition: Prism

A transparent object with triangular cross-section that refracts and disperses light.

Diagram: Prism Refraction

Prism Formula:

δ = (i₁ + i₂) - A

Where: δ = angle of deviation, i₁ = angle of incidence, i₂ = angle of emergence, A = angle of prism

6. Dispersion of White Light by a Glass Prism

Dispersion is the splitting of white light into its constituent colors.

Definition: Dispersion

The splitting of white light into its constituent colors when passed through a prism.

Colors of Spectrum (VIBGYOR):

  • Violet (highest frequency, shortest wavelength)
  • Indigo
  • Blue
  • Green
  • Yellow
  • Orange
  • Red (lowest frequency, longest wavelength)

Diagram: Dispersion of Light

7. Atmospheric Refraction

Atmospheric refraction causes various optical phenomena in the atmosphere.

Definition: Atmospheric Refraction

The bending of light rays as they pass through layers of air with different densities.

Effects of Atmospheric Refraction:

  • Twinkling of stars
  • Advanced sunrise and delayed sunset
  • Apparent position of stars
  • Mirage formation

Example: Twinkling of Stars

Stars appear to twinkle because their light passes through different layers of air with varying densities, causing the light to bend continuously.

8. Scattering of Light

Scattering is the phenomenon where light is redirected in all directions by particles.

Definition: Scattering

The phenomenon where light is redirected in all directions by particles in its path.

Types of Scattering:

  • Rayleigh Scattering: Scattering by particles smaller than wavelength
  • Tyndall Effect: Scattering by colloidal particles

Examples of Scattering:

  • Blue color of sky
  • Red color of sunset
  • White color of clouds
  • Visibility of beam of light in fog

Scattering Formula:

I ∝ 1/λ⁴

Where: I = intensity of scattered light, λ = wavelength
