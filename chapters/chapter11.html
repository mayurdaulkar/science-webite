1. The Human Eye

The human eye is a complex optical instrument that enables us to see the world around us.

Definition: Human Eye

A complex optical system that forms images on the retina and enables vision.

Parts of the Human Eye:

  • Cornea: Transparent front part that refracts light
  • Iris: Colored part that controls pupil size
  • Pupil: Central opening that allows light to enter
  • Lens: Biconvex structure that focuses light
  • Retina: Light-sensitive layer with photoreceptors
  • Optic Nerve: Carries visual information to brain

Diagram: Human Eye Structure

Functions of Eye Parts:

  • Cornea: Bends light rays to focus them
  • Iris: Controls amount of light entering eye
  • Lens: Changes shape to focus on objects
  • Retina: Contains rods and cones for vision
  • Optic Nerve: Transmits visual signals to brain

2. Defects of Vision and their Correction

Various defects can affect normal vision and require corrective measures.

Definition: Vision Defects

Conditions that prevent the eye from forming clear images on the retina.

Common Vision Defects:

  • Myopia (Nearsightedness): Can see near objects clearly
  • Hypermetropia (Farsightedness): Can see distant objects clearly
  • Presbyopia: Age-related loss of near vision
  • Astigmatism: Irregular curvature of cornea

Diagram: Vision Defects

Power of Lens:

P = 1/f (in diopters)

Where: P = power, f = focal length in meters

Example: Myopia Correction

A person with myopia has a far point at 2 meters. The power of the concave lens needed is P = 1/(-2) = -0.5 D.

3. Refraction of Light through a Prism

A prism is a transparent object with triangular cross-section that disperses light.

Definition: Prism

A transparent object with triangular cross-section that refracts and disperses light.

Diagram: Prism Refraction

Prism Formula:

δ = (i₁ + i₂) - A

Where: δ = angle of deviation, i₁ = angle of incidence, i₂ = angle of emergence, A = angle of prism

4. Dispersion of White Light by a Glass Prism

Dispersion is the splitting of white light into its constituent colors.

Definition: Dispersion

The splitting of white light into its constituent colors when passed through a prism.

Colors of Spectrum (VIBGYOR):

  • Violet (highest frequency, shortest wavelength)
  • Indigo
  • Blue
  • Green
  • Yellow
  • Orange
  • Red (lowest frequency, longest wavelength)

5. Atmospheric Refraction

Atmospheric refraction causes various optical phenomena in the atmosphere.

Definition: Atmospheric Refraction

The bending of light rays as they pass through layers of air with different densities.

Effects of Atmospheric Refraction:

  • Twinkling of stars
  • Advanced sunrise and delayed sunset
  • Apparent position of stars
  • Mirage formation

Diagram: Atmospheric Refraction

Example: Twinkling of Stars

Stars appear to twinkle because their light passes through different layers of air with varying densities, causing the light to bend continuously.

6. Scattering of Light

Scattering is the phenomenon where light is redirected in all directions by particles.

Definition: Scattering

The phenomenon where light is redirected in all directions by particles in its path.

Types of Scattering:

  • Rayleigh Scattering: Scattering by particles smaller than wavelength
  • Tyndall Effect: Scattering by colloidal particles

Examples of Scattering:

  • Blue color of sky
  • Red color of sunset
  • White color of clouds
  • Visibility of beam of light in fog

Diagram: Scattering of Light

Scattering Formula:

I ∝ 1/λ⁴

Where: I = intensity of scattered light, λ = wavelength
