Chapter 3: Metals and Non-metals

Understanding properties and reactivity of metals and non-metals

Key Topics

  • Physical Properties of Metals and Non-metals
  • Chemical Properties of Metals
  • Reactivity Series
  • Extraction of Metals
  • Corrosion and its Prevention

1. Physical Properties of Metals and Non-metals

Properties of Metals:

  • Lustrous: Shiny appearance
  • Malleable: Can be beaten into sheets
  • Ductile: Can be drawn into wires
  • Good conductors: Heat and electricity
  • Sonorous: Produce sound when struck
  • High melting and boiling points

Properties of Non-metals:

  • Dull appearance
  • Brittle: Break easily
  • Poor conductors: Heat and electricity
  • Low melting and boiling points
  • Non-sonorous
Examples:

Metals: Iron, Copper, Aluminium, Gold, Silver

Non-metals: Carbon, Sulphur, Oxygen, Nitrogen, Chlorine

2. Chemical Properties of Metals

1. Reaction with Oxygen:

Metal + Oxygen → Metal Oxide
Examples:
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃

2. Reaction with Water:

Metal + Water → Metal Hydroxide + Hydrogen
Examples:
2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂
Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂

3. Reaction with Acids:

Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen
Examples:
Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂
Fe + H₂SO₄ → FeSO₄ + H₂

Important Note

Not all metals react with acids. Noble metals like gold, silver, and platinum do not react with acids.

3. Reactivity Series

Reactivity Series

An arrangement of metals in order of their decreasing reactivity towards oxygen, water, and acids.

Reactivity Series (Most Reactive to Least Reactive):

K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Sn > Pb > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au

Memory Aid: "Please Stop Calling Me A Zebra, I Like Snakes, Pythons, Cats, Horses, And Goldfish"

Applications of Reactivity Series:

  • Displacement Reactions: More reactive metals can displace less reactive metals from their compounds
  • Extraction of Metals: Metals can be extracted from their ores using more reactive metals
  • Corrosion: More reactive metals corrode faster
Example:
Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu
(Iron displaces copper because Fe is more reactive than Cu)

4. Extraction of Metals

Methods of Extraction:

1. Electrolysis (Most Reactive Metals):

Used for highly reactive metals like K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al

Example: 2Al₂O₃ → 4Al + 3O₂ (Electrolysis of alumina)

2. Reduction with Carbon (Medium Reactive Metals):

Used for metals like Zn, Fe, Pb, Cu

Example: ZnO + C → Zn + CO

3. Roasting and Reduction (Less Reactive Metals):

Used for metals like Hg, Ag

Example: HgS + O₂ → Hg + SO₂

Key Points

  • More reactive metals require more energy for extraction
  • Carbon can reduce oxides of metals below it in reactivity series
  • Electrolysis is expensive but necessary for reactive metals

5. Corrosion and its Prevention

Corrosion

Gradual destruction of metals by chemical reaction with their environment.

Common Types of Corrosion:

  • Rusting: Iron + Oxygen + Water → Rust
  • Tarnishing: Silver + Hydrogen Sulphide → Silver Sulphide
  • Patina: Copper + Oxygen + Carbon Dioxide → Green coating

Prevention Methods:

  • Galvanization: Coating with zinc
  • Electroplating: Coating with another metal
  • Alloying: Mixing with other metals
  • Painting/Oiling: Protective coating
  • Cathodic Protection: Using sacrificial anode
Example:
Rusting: 4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃
Prevention: Zn + Fe → Zn corrodes, Fe protected

Important Formulas and Definitions

Key Points to Remember

  • Metals are good conductors of heat and electricity
  • Non-metals are poor conductors
  • Reactivity series helps predict displacement reactions
  • More reactive metals are harder to extract
  • Corrosion can be prevented by various methods

Important Chemical Equations:

  • Metal + Oxygen → Metal Oxide
  • Metal + Water → Metal Hydroxide + Hydrogen
  • Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen
  • More Reactive Metal + Less Reactive Metal Salt → More Reactive Metal Salt + Less Reactive Metal
